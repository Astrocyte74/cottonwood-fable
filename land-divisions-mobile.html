The Dominion Land Survey · 1871–1930

How the Prairies Were Squared

I Why a grid

A continent turned into property

In 1870 Canada bought Rupert’s Land and needed to turn it into farms it could sell. The fix: one giant rectangular survey, and a deal — 160 acres for a $10 filing fee, yours after three years of work.

II Where counting starts

Meridians & the baseline

Townships count north of the 49th parallel; ranges count east or west of a meridian. Cottonwood sits just west of the 5th — that’s the “W5”. Tap a meridian.

III Six miles square

36 sections, counted as a snake

Section 1 starts at the south-east corner, runs west, then snakes back east row by row to Section 36. The same path the map draws.

VI The mile grid

Why the roads run dead straight

A 66-foot road allowance was reserved along the south & west of every section — so range roads and township roads still trace the 1871 survey.

VII A round Earth

The jog in the road

converge more

Meridians pinch together going north, so a correction line every 24 miles resets them — leaving the offset you feel where a road jogs. Cottonwood’s is the T36/T37 line.

VIII Reserved before settlers

The checkerboard of owners

Schools got 11 & 29; the Hudson’s Bay Co. got 8 & 26; railways took the odd sections. On the Cottonwood sheet: Sec 11 = school, Sec 7–9 = H.B.C., odd = C.P.R./C&E.

IX What the grid overran

The land order it displaced

Along the rivers, the Métis river-lot system already divided land into narrow ribbons reaching back from the water. When surveyors arrived at Red River in 1869, Métis residents turned them away — sparking the Red River Resistance.

X The grid beneath Cottonwood

Townships 35–36, Ranges 2–3, W5 — settled by Robertson, Laird, Caldwell and, just west, the Danish community at Dickson. The map traces who held each quarter across 1901–1927.
