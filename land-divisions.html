The Dominion Land Survey · 1871–1930

How the Survey Works

A grid drawn across half a continent decided who farmed where — and still shapes every road, field, and title beneath the Cottonwood district today.

↓

IA continent, converted to property

In 1870 Canada bought Rupert’s Land from the Hudson’s Bay Company and needed a way to turn a fur-trade empire into farms it could sell.

The answer was the Dominion Land Survey (DLS): one enormous rectangular grid laid over the prairies so any parcel could be named, sold, taxed, and granted. The Dominion Lands Act of 1872 made the deal explicit — a settler could file on a quarter-section of 160 acres for a $10 fee, then earn the title (“patent”) after three years of living on and cultivating it.

It was never just engineering. Surveying the land meant financing railways with it, reserving sections for schools, and — where it overran older ways of holding land — displacing the people already there. The grid was a nation-building project drawn in chains and stakes.

IIWhere you start counting

The whole grid hangs off two reference lines: the 49th parallel at the Canada–U.S. border, and a series of principal meridians running north.

Townships are counted north from the 49th parallel baseline. Ranges are counted east or west of the nearest principal meridian. Six meridians step westward across the prairies; the Cottonwood district sits just west of the Fifth Meridian (114° W) — hence the “W5” that ends every legal description here.

IIIThe township: six miles square

Each township is a 6×6 mile block holding 36 sections, every section one square mile (640 acres).

Section numbering snakes — west along the bottom tier, east along the next, and so on to 36.

The sections aren’t numbered left-to-right. They follow a boustrophedon (“as the ox ploughs”) path: Section 1 starts in the south-east corner, numbering runs west across the bottom row, then east across the next row up, snaking back and forth until Section 36 lands in the north-east corner.

Knowing the snake lets you find any section by eye — and it’s exactly the pattern the Cottonwood map draws. Watch it count.

IVDividing the square

A section splits into quarters for homesteaders, and again into legal subdivisions for the oil patch.

One section = 640 acres. Cut it in four and you get the quarter-sections — NW, NE, SW, SE, 160 acres each. That quarter was the classic homestead: enough land for a family, small enough to prove up.

Cut the section into sixteen instead and you get Legal Subdivisions (LSDs), about 40 acres each — numbered 1–16 in the same snaking pattern. An LSD is a sixteenth of a section, not a quarter (a common mix-up). Today they pinpoint oil and gas wells.

One square mile — 640 acres.

VReading a legal land description

Stack those units in order and you get an address that has located prairie land for 150 years: quarter – section – township – range – meridian.

The classic textbook example is NW‑14‑23‑4‑W5 — the north-west quarter of section 14, township 23, range 4, west of the 5th Meridian. The very same convention still appears on Alberta land titles, oil-and-gas leases, and rural addresses. The builder above starts on a real Cottonwood parcel.

VIWhy the roads run dead straight

A one-chain (66 ft) road allowance was reserved along the south and west of every section.

Canada modified the American survey in one very visible way: it baked road allowances — strips one chain (66 feet) wide — into the grid from the start, running along the south and west edge of each section.

That single rule is why central Alberta is a vast mile-grid of rural roads, and why range roads (running north–south) and township roads (east–west) line up so precisely with section boundaries. The roads around Cottonwood — Township Roads 352, 354, 355, 360 and Range Roads 24, 25, 30, 31, 32 — are the 1871 survey, paved.

VIIThe jog in the road

The Earth is round, so a perfect northward grid can’t stay rectangular. The fix leaves a kink you can still drive over.

Meridians converge toward the pole, so range lines drawn straight north would slowly squeeze together. To keep sections close to a square mile, the survey inserts a correction line every 24 miles (every four townships), where the range lines jump back west to their proper spacing.

The result is the little offset where a road suddenly fails to line up across a township boundary — familiar to anyone driving the prairies. In the study area these fall at the Township 28, 32, 36 and 40 lines; the T36/T37 boundary is the correction line along Cottonwood’s northern edge.

VIIIThe checkerboard of owners

Not every section was a homestead. Specific sections were reserved before a settler ever arrived — an interlocking checkerboard of schools, the Hudson’s Bay Company, and railways.

Toggle the reservations on this township. Settlers got what was left.

School sections (11 & 29). Two sections per township were held back; the proceeds of their eventual sale funded schools. By 1913 that was 8.5 million acres — 373 townships’ worth — across the three prairie provinces.

Hudson’s Bay Company (8 & part of 26). The 1870 transfer gave the HBC one-twentieth of the fertile belt, taken as section 8 and three-quarters of section 26 in each township.

Railway grants (odd sections). Railways were paid in land — alternating odd-numbered sections in a belt along the line. Around Cottonwood the relevant grant was the Calgary & Edmonton Railway, whose Siding 7 became the town of Bowden in 1891.

On the Cottonwood sheet you can read this directly: Section 11 is the school section, the Hudson’s Bay Company holds Sections 7–9 in Range 2, and the C.P.R./C&E took the odd sections.

IXThe land order the grid overran

The square survey did not fall on an empty map. Along the Red and Assiniboine rivers the Métis river-lot system was already in place — dating to 1813 — dividing land into narrow ribbons running back from the water, so every household had river frontage, transport, and shared hay and timber.

That logic was incompatible with standardized rectangular parcels. When Canadian surveyors arrived at Red River in 1869, Métis residents turned them away, helping trigger the Red River Resistance. The square grid threatened not just parcel shapes but community authority — and the scrip process that followed often dispossessed Métis families rather than securing their land.

XThe grid beneath Cottonwood

The survey never went away. In Alberta it lives on as the Alberta Township System — the backbone of land titles, well locations, and county roads.

Search Alberta land today and you still search by quarter, section, township, range, and meridian. The Cottonwood & Gleniffer district — Townships 35–36, Ranges 2–3, W5 — is a settled prairie block whose roads, school sections and ownership pattern came straight from the 1871 survey.

Its names survive in the record: William Robertson, the Gleniffer postmaster from Paisley, Scotland, who gave Gleniffer Lake its name; James Alexander Laird, who homesteaded Moose Ridge in 1903; Augustus Caldwell of the Mountain House post office; and, just west, the Danish settlement at Dickson, founded by Carl Christiansen and 17 compatriots in 1903.
