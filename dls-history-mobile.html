I Before the grid

An empire of fur

In 1670 the Hudson’s Bay Company was granted Rupert’s Land — ~40% of modern Canada — and ran it for furs, not farms, for two centuries. No townships, no section lines.

II 1868–70

Buying a third of a country

The HBC ceded its territory for £300,000, keeping its posts and one-twentieth of the fertile belt — which became sections 8 & 26 in every township. The transfer took effect 15 July 1870.

IV The first attempt

Dennis & the eight-mile township

J.S. Dennis studied the U.S. system in St. Paul and first proposed 8-mile townships of 64 sections. It was swapped for the American 640-acre / 6-mile township — which yielded far more homesteads.

V 1869

The survey that sparked a resistance

The American-style lines threatened Métis river lots. On 11 Oct 1869, Métis under Louis Riel halted the survey on André Nault’s farm.

“[They] are handed over like a flock of sheep to us; and they are told that they lose their lands.” — Sir John A. Macdonald, privately
VI 1871

The system is set

Dennis fixes the final plan — 6-mile townships, 36 sections, 640 acres. The first survey post is driven near Headingley on 10 July 1871.

VII 14 April 1872

The Dominion Lands Act

160 acres for $10 to any household head or man 18+ — patented after 3 years’ residence, 30 acres broken, and a dwelling built. The fee & cultivation rule fought speculation.

VIII The fieldwork

How it was actually done

Parties of up to 20 worked a roadless prairie with a solar compass (true north from the sun). Their field notes and township plans became the legal basis of every title.

IX 1896–1914

The Last Best West

For 25 years almost no one came. Then Clifford Sifton marketed Canada as the “Last Best West” — immigration leapt from 16,835 (1896) to 141,465 (1905).

The reckoning

What the grid really was

A sovereignty project over land already inhabited — displacing Indigenous and Métis land orders — and, at the same time, the same 160 acres and $10 in theory open to anyone who showed up and stayed.

Part Two

The American Blueprint

Canada didn’t invent the grid — it borrowed a system already 86 years old, and fixed the problems the Americans had hit.

XI 1785

A system 86 years older

The U.S. Public Land Survey System (1785) is the DLS’s blueprint — same 6-mile township, 36 sections, 160-acre quarters. One quirk differs:

U.S. starts Section 1 in the NE corner; Canada in the SE. Both then snake.

Survey first, settle second

Two ways to divide land

Parcels that follow the landscape, versus identical squares imposed before anyone arrives — the choice Canada made everywhere.

XIII Same skeleton

The differences that mattered

🇨🇦 Dominion Land Survey

  • Road allowances built in (66 ft on every section).
  • 6 meridians, evenly spaced — one clean system.
  • Layered reserves: school, HBC, railway.
  • Section 1 in the south-east.

🇺🇸 Public Land Survey System

  • No road allowances — access negotiated later.
  • 37 meridians, set piecemeal.
  • School sections only (16 & 36).
  • Section 1 in the north-east.
XIV From the air

Clean grid, patchwork nation

Canada surveyed its west as one federal grid before settlement — ~200M acres, still operative today. The U.S. patched the PLSS over metes-and-bounds and Spanish land grants. The prairie is the purest rectangular survey on Earth.
